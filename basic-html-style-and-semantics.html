Text-Based Elements

Style v. Semantics

strong and em are inline-level elements and the more popular choices.

There are semantic differences between strong and b and em and i even though they stylistically appear the same on a browser.

Therefore, you have to gauge the significance of the text we wish to set as bold or italics and to choose an element accordingly

Bold:

strong is bold with a strong importance

b is bold with an inline stylistic offset

Italics:

em is italics with a stressed emphasis

i is italics with an alternative voice or tone (as if placed in quotation marks)

Structural Elements

Choosing between div, section, and article:

div is for dividers that are purely stylistic. articles and sections are a bit trickier.

If the content adds to the document outline and represents a thematic group of content, use the section element.

If the content adds to the document outline and be independently redistributed or syndicated, use the article element.

nav identifies a section of major navigational links on a page

asideholds content, such as sidebars, inserts, or brief explanations, that is tangentially related to the content surrounding it

When used within an article> element, for example, the aside element may identify content related to the author of the article.

Remember: all of the structural elements, including the aside element, are block-level elements and as such will appear on a new line, occupying the full available width of the page or of the element they are nested within, also known as their parent element.

The footer element identifies the closing or end of a page, article, section, or other segment of a page.

Footers are generally found at the bottom of their parent, and the content within it should be relevant to and not diverge from the document or section it is included within.
